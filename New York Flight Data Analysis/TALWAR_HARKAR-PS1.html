Preparing data for visualizations

In [20]:
#Prepare data for Plot 1 (Box plot for airline vs departure delays)
#Extract the series of unique airlines
carriers_unique = flights_df['carrier'].unique()
carrier_delays = []
for carrier in carriers_unique:
    #Extract non-null delays for each airline into a new list
    carrier_delays.append(list(flights_df[np.logical_and(flights_df['carrier'] == carrier, 
                                                         pd.notnull(flights_df['dep_delay']))]['dep_delay']))

#Prepare data for Plot 2 (Average departure delays by airline)   
carriers_avg_delays = pd.DataFrame(flights_df.groupby(['carrier']).dep_delay.mean())
carriers_avg_delays.reset_index(level = 0, inplace = True)

#Prepare data for Plot 3 (Percent of flights delayed by airline)
#Extract count of flights by airline
flights_count_airline = pd.DataFrame(flights_df.groupby(['carrier']).flight.count())
flights_count_airline.reset_index(level = 0, inplace = True)
#Extract count of delayed flights by airline
flights_delaycount_airline = pd.DataFrame(flights_df[np.logical_and(pd.notnull(flights_df['dep_delay']), 
                                                                    flights_df['dep_delay'] > 0)]
                                          .groupby(['carrier']).flight.count())
flights_delaycount_airline.reset_index(level = 0, inplace = True)
#Merge the data for the two counts into a single dataframe
carrier_counts = pd.merge(flights_count_airline, flights_delaycount_airline, on='carrier')
carrier_counts.rename(columns={'flight_x': 'total_count', 'flight_y': 'delay_count'}, inplace=True)
#Compute the percent of flights delayed based on the above two counts
carrier_counts['percent_delays'] = carrier_counts['delay_count']/carrier_counts['total_count'] * 100

  1. In terms of the average departure delays (plot 2 above), airline F9 (Frontier Airlines) seems to be doing the worst, closely matched by airline EV (Atlantic Southeast Airlines).
  2. However, looking at the box plot, the airline EV seems to be more unpredicatable than F9 in terms of the delays, showing more variability in adherance to departure times. Consistent performance could be an important factor for passengers.
  3. Looking at the last plot however, we see that the airline WN (Southwest Airlines), beats both EV and F9, and has the highest percentage of flights delayed, out of the total flights operated.

Therefore, there doesn't seem to be one clear worst performing airline. Depending on the criteria, we have different candidates for poor performance in terms of departure delays, namely, Atlantic Southeast Airlines, Frontier Airlines, and Southwest Airlines

The above plots are in agreement with what the correlations suggested:

  1. There is a positive correlation between humidity and average departure delay, with days having lower humidity levels showing lower delay times.
  2. There is a slight negative assocation of the departure delays with visibility, which one would expect intuitively. Days having higher (good visibility) reported lower departure delay times.
  3. For precipitation, most of the data points are cluttered in the area of low precipitation values, with a lot of variation in departure delays, therefore it seems hard to establish an association.
  4. Lastly, the average departure delays seem to increase slightly, as the dew point increases.

In our analysis earlier, we had found that the months expected to have high temperatures (June and July) had the highest average departure delays. Moreover, we found above that the dew point has a positive correlation with departure delays as well as with temperature. Therefore, it can be worthwhile to further explore month-wise delays against temperature.
